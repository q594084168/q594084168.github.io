Why Request a Department Transfer Matters in the Workplace

Leadership communication carries outsized weight. When a senior leader sends an email, every word is scrutinized, forwarded, and interpreted through multiple lenses. A poorly phrased piece of feedback can demoralize a high performer. A vague announcement during uncertain times can fuel anxiety. Conversely, clear, empathetic leadership communication builds trust that compounds over time.

The challenge is that most leaders receive zero formal training in written communication. They're promoted for technical or strategic skills, then expected to intuitively know how to give tough feedback or announce difficult news through writing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I address poor performance with an underperforming team member?

Be specific about what 'good performance' looks like versus what you're observing. Use concrete examples and their impact on the team. Ask 'What's getting in the way of you meeting these expectations?' — sometimes there's a legitimate obstacle you don't know about. Create a written improvement plan with measurable goals and regular check-ins. Document everything for both accountability and support purposes.

How do I announce bad news to my team without killing morale?

Be transparent about what happened, what you know, and what you're still figuring out. Share what you're doing about it and what specific support is available to the team. Then give space for questions — don't rush through the uncomfortable part. Teams handle uncertainty better when their leader shows vulnerability and communicates a clear, even if imperfect, path forward.

How do I give tough feedback to a high-performing employee?

Separate the person from the specific behavior. Start with genuine appreciation for their strengths and contributions — this isn't flattery, it's context. Then address the specific behavior that needs to change using concrete examples, not generalizations. End with confidence in their ability to improve and an offer of support. High performers usually prefer direct candor over sugarcoating — they want to know what they need to fix.

How do I write a subject line that actually gets a response?

Be specific and action-oriented. Instead of 'Meeting,' write 'Decision needed: Q3 budget proposal by Friday.' Instead of 'Hello,' write 'Quick question about the Johnson account renewal.' The subject line should immediately tell the recipient: (1) what this is about, (2) what action is needed from them, and (3) when. Aim for under 50 characters for mobile readability.

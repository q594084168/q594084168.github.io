Frequently Asked Questions

What's the right way to call in sick via email?

Keep it brief, professional, and free of unnecessary medical details. State that you're unwell and unable to work today. Indicate whether you expect to return tomorrow or need more time. Flag any urgent items that need coverage while you're out. No one needs to know your symptoms — 'I'm not feeling well' is sufficient and appropriate in any professional context.

How can I write emails that people actually read?

Put the most important information first — journalists call this the 'inverted pyramid.' Use short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max). Bold key dates, action items, and deadlines so they're visible even when scanning. If your email requires a response, ask for it explicitly and set a clear deadline. Avoid long background sections before getting to the point — busy recipients will stop reading before they reach your ask.

How do I request a meeting with a busy executive?

Be ruthlessly concise. State the purpose of the meeting in one sentence. Propose a specific duration (15 minutes is usually more acceptable than 30). Offer multiple time slots to make scheduling easy. Explain in one sentence what they'll get out of the meeting — busy executives want to understand the value of their time before committing it.

Should I use emojis in professional emails?

It depends entirely on your workplace culture, your relationship with the recipient, and the context. In formal external communication with clients or executives, avoid them entirely — they can undermine your professionalism. In internal Slack messages with close colleagues you chat with daily, a well-placed emoji can actually soften your tone and prevent messages from sounding colder than intended. When in doubt, leave them out.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do if I haven't heard back after a final interview?

If a week has passed beyond the stated timeline, send a brief follow-up. Reference something specific from your conversation to show you were engaged, and ask if there's a revised timeline. If you still get no response after two attempts, it may be time to mentally move on — but keep the door open with a gracious final note.

How do I follow up after a job application without being annoying?

Wait 5-7 business days after submitting your application before following up. Keep your message concise: reference the specific position title, briefly reiterate your interest, and ask if there's anything else you can provide to support their decision. Avoid filler phrases like 'just checking in' — they add no value and make you sound unsure. A good follow-up email demonstrates persistence without desperation, which hiring managers actually respect.

Is it OK to negotiate salary via email?

Yes, especially for remote positions where email is the primary communication channel. Email actually gives you an advantage — it gives you time to craft your argument carefully with market data and specific examples of your value. Start by expressing genuine enthusiasm for the role, then present your counter-offer with supporting evidence, and end by reaffirming your excitement to join the team.

How can I write emails that people actually read?

Put the most important information first — journalists call this the 'inverted pyramid.' Use short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max). Bold key dates, action items, and deadlines so they're visible even when scanning. If your email requires a response, ask for it explicitly and set a clear deadline. Avoid long background sections before getting to the point — busy recipients will stop reading before they reach your ask.

Frequently Asked Questions

What tone should I use when asking for a raise?

Be confident but not entitled. Frame the request around the measurable value you've delivered — cite specific projects, revenue impact, or efficiency gains you've contributed. Schedule a dedicated meeting rather than slipping the request into a casual conversation. Prepare talking points in advance, and be ready to discuss market salary data for your role. Remember: you're making a business case, not asking for a favor.

How do I follow up after a job application without being annoying?

Wait 5-7 business days after submitting your application before following up. Keep your message concise: reference the specific position title, briefly reiterate your interest, and ask if there's anything else you can provide to support their decision. Avoid filler phrases like 'just checking in' — they add no value and make you sound unsure. A good follow-up email demonstrates persistence without desperation, which hiring managers actually respect.

What's the ideal length for a professional email?

Five sentences or fewer is the gold standard for most business emails. If you genuinely need more detail, use bullet points to make the content scannable. The critical rule: your main request or question should be visible in the preview pane of any email client — that means it must appear in the first 2-3 lines. People scan emails on their phones during meetings; if your key message is buried in paragraph three, it probably won't be read at all.

How long should I wait before following up after an interview?

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours of your interview — this is non-negotiable. If the interviewer gave you a timeline for decisions and it has passed, wait an additional 3-5 business days before sending a polite follow-up. Keep your tone gracious and understanding: acknowledge they're busy, reaffirm your enthusiasm, and ask if there's any additional information you can provide.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the ideal length for a professional email?

Five sentences or fewer is the gold standard for most business emails. If you genuinely need more detail, use bullet points to make the content scannable. The critical rule: your main request or question should be visible in the preview pane of any email client — that means it must appear in the first 2-3 lines. People scan emails on their phones during meetings; if your key message is buried in paragraph three, it probably won't be read at all.

What if a coworker takes credit for my work?

Address it promptly but professionally. In a private conversation, say 'I noticed the project update highlighted X — I'd appreciate if we could acknowledge the team's contributions more evenly going forward.' As a preventive measure, document your work visibly — use shared project trackers, send update emails to relevant stakeholders, and present your contributions in team meetings so your ownership is clear to everyone, not just the credit-taker.

How do I say 'that's not my job' professionally?

Don't use those exact words. Instead, redirect constructively: 'I believe that falls under [specific department or role]. I'd recommend checking with [name] who handles that area — they'd have the most up-to-date information.' If it's something you could do but shouldn't be responsible for, add: 'I'm happy to help this time, but going forward, could we clarify whose scope this falls under?'

How do I deal with a micromanaging boss?

Proactively communicate. Send brief daily or weekly status updates before they ask — this alone often reduces their anxiety-driven need to check in. If the behavior persists, request a private conversation about working styles. Frame it as a request for more autonomy so you can deliver better results, not as a complaint. Most micromanagers are anxious, not malicious, and giving them information voluntarily reduces their urge to seek it.

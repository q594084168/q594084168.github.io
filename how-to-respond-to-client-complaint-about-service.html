Why Respond to Client Service Complaint Matters in the Workplace

Money conversations are uncomfortable by design. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I ask a client for payment without damaging the relationship?

Frame your reminder as helpful rather than accusatory. Start with a friendly opener referencing your recent work together, then mention that you 'wanted to make sure the invoice didn't get lost in their inbox.' Attach the original invoice again for their convenience, and offer to discuss payment terms or schedules if they're experiencing any difficulties. The key is to make it easy for them to pay while preserving the professional relationship.

How can I write emails that people actually read?

Put the most important information first — journalists call this the 'inverted pyramid.' Use short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max). Bold key dates, action items, and deadlines so they're visible even when scanning. If your email requires a response, ask for it explicitly and set a clear deadline. Avoid long background sections before getting to the point — busy recipients will stop reading before they reach your ask.

What if a client keeps making excuses for late payment?

After the second missed deadline, escalate your communication from friendly reminders to firm expectations. Set a specific final payment date, and clearly state (without threats) that you'll need to pause ongoing work until the balance is cleared. Document every communication — timestamps, responses, and promises made. Most reasonable clients respect clearly communicated boundaries, and the ones who don't were going to be problems anyway.

How do I tell a client they are wrong without offending them?

Lead with curiosity, not accusation. Instead of saying 'You're wrong about X,' try 'I may be looking at this differently — could we walk through the data together?' This positions you as a collaborative problem-solver rather than a critic. Always present evidence neutrally and focus on shared goals rather than who made the mistake. A well-handled correction can actually strengthen client trust.
